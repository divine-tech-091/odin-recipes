Cuisine Page

Description

Chinese food offers an immense variety of regional flavors, ranging from Sichuan's spicy dishes like Mapo Tofu and Kung Pao Chicken to Cantonese Dim Sum and steamed Baozi.

Ingredients

  • Aromatics: Fresh ginger, garlic, and green onions (scallions).
  • Sauces & Condiments: Light soy sauce (for salt/umami), dark soy sauce (for color/depth), oyster sauce, hoisin sauce, and chili paste or chili crisp.
  • Cooking Wine & Oils: Shaoxing wine (or dry sherry) and toasted sesame oil.
  • Thickeners & Spices: Cornstarch (for thickening and velveting meat) and white pepper.
  • Flavorings: Sugar, chicken powder/MSG, and five-spice powder.

Steps

  1. Knife Skills: Use a sharp knife for precision and safety.
  2. Layering Flavors: Start with aromatics (garlic, onions, herbs) and build flavors throughout the cooking process.
  3. Homemade Stocks: Create deeper, richer flavors by making your own stocks, rather than using store-bought.
  4. Season as You Go: Taste frequently and adjust seasoning (salt, spices) thoughtfully to avoid over- or under-seasoning.
  5. Heat Management: Use the correct temperature; for example, ensure oil is hot enough ( ) for frying to avoid soggy food.
